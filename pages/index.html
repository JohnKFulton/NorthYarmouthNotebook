Understanding Ranked Choice Voting

🗳️

How It Works

Voters rank candidates by preference (1st, 2nd, 3rd choice). If no candidate wins a majority in the first round, the lowest-performing candidate is eliminated and their votes are redistributed to voters' next preferences.

✨

Key Benefits

RCV ensures the winner has majority support, gives voters more meaningful choices, reduces "vote splitting," and allows minority voices to be heard without being "wasted."

🏛️

Maine's Implementation

Maine adopted RCV for federal elections in 2018 and expanded it to gubernatorial primaries. This automatic runoff system ensures winners have demonstrated majority support.

What happens if a ballot doesn't have a second choice? ▶

That ballot is considered "exhausted" and removed from future rounds. This is why the simulations allow you to specify the percentage of votes that become exhausted.

Why use these simulations? ▶

These tools let you explore different "what-if" scenarios. By adjusting how votes transfer between candidates, you can understand how different voter preferences would affect the final outcome.

How do I use the simulators? ▶

Click "Launch Democratic/Republican Simulator" above. When a candidate is eliminated, use the sliders to allocate their votes to other candidates. Try the "Equal Split" preset for balanced distribution or "Opponent Consolidation" to see strategic voting.
